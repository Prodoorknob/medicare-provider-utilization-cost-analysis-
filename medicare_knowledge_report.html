Section 01

What is Medicare?

Medicare is the United States federal health insurance program, created in 1965. It primarily covers people who are 65 or older, along with certain younger people with disabilities and individuals with End-Stage Renal Disease (permanent kidney failure). As of 2024, roughly 67 million Americans are enrolled.

It is funded through a combination of payroll taxes (the 1.45% Medicare tax on every paycheck), beneficiary premiums, and general federal revenue. Unlike private insurance, Medicare is not run by a single insurer — CMS (Centers for Medicare & Medicaid Services) sets the rules, but regional Medicare Administrative Contractors (MACs) actually process claims.

Why this matters for your models

Medicare is the single largest payer of healthcare services in the United States. The fee schedules Medicare sets are used as reference points by virtually every private insurer. Understanding how Medicare prices services is foundational to understanding American healthcare costs at large.

Fee-for-Service (FFS) vs. Medicare Advantage

There are two fundamentally different ways to receive Medicare. Traditional/Original Medicare (also called Fee-for-Service) is what your entire CMS dataset covers — the government pays providers directly for each service rendered. Medicare Advantage (Part C) is where a beneficiary enrolls in a private insurance plan that bundles Medicare benefits, often with extra perks. CMS pays the private plan a fixed monthly rate, and the plan manages the claims internally.

As of 2024, approximately 54% of Medicare beneficiaries are enrolled in Medicare Advantage — meaning your CMS dataset only represents about half of all Medicare patients. This is the single most important structural limitation to state in your project.

HCPCS Codes — the DNA of your dataset

Every service in Part B is billed using a HCPCS code (Healthcare Common Procedure Coding System, pronounced "hick-picks"). Think of it as a SKU for medical services. "99213" means a standard office visit. "93000" means an electrocardiogram. Your entire prediction target — the allowed amount — is set at the HCPCS code level, adjusted for geography and modifiers.

Critical concept — the deductible complication

Before Medicare pays anything, the beneficiary must meet their annual Part B deductible ($240 in 2024). A patient visiting in January who hasn't yet met their deductible pays the full allowed amount themselves. The same patient in December — deductible already met — pays only 20%. This time-within-year dynamic is one reason patient out-of-pocket is so difficult to predict without beneficiary-level data.

Provider participation status

Not all providers play by the same rules. There are three tiers, and they determine how much a patient can be billed above the allowed amount:

StatusWhat It MeansMax Patient Charge
ParticipatingAccepts Medicare assignment — agrees to the allowed amount as full payment20% of allowed amount
Non-ParticipatingDoesn't accept assignment but still must bill through MedicareUp to 115% of allowed amount ("limiting charge")
Opted-OutCompletely outside Medicare — patients pay out-of-pocket entirely, Medicare reimburses nothingWhatever is privately negotiated

The vast majority of physicians (~90%) are participating providers, which is why the 80/20 split is the standard mental model. Your CMS dataset only includes participating and non-participating providers — opted-out providers don't submit claims to Medicare at all.

Section 04

Key Terminology Your Models Depend On

These are the terms that appear directly in your dataset or are foundational to interpreting your model outputs.

NPI — National Provider Identifier
A unique 10-digit number assigned to every healthcare provider in the US. Your dataset is organized at the NPI level — one row per provider × HCPCS code × place of service.
→ The unit of observation in your CMS data
HCPCS Code
The procedure code that identifies what service was performed. CPT codes (physician services) are a subset of HCPCS. This is the primary categorical feature in your models alongside specialty.
→ 99213 = Office visit, 93000 = ECG
Allowed Amount
The maximum amount Medicare will pay for a service, combining both the Medicare payment and the patient cost-sharing portion. This is your Stage 1 prediction target.
→ Avg_Medicare_Allowed_Amt in your dataset
Geographic Practice Cost Index (GPCI)
A set of adjustment factors that modify the Medicare fee schedule based on local costs of practicing medicine — labor, office space, and malpractice. This is why the same procedure pays differently in Manhattan vs. rural Indiana.
→ Explains ~20–30% of geographic variation
Place of Service (POS)
Indicates whether the service was delivered in a facility (hospital outpatient) or non-facility (private office) setting. Medicare pays different rates for the same HCPCS code depending on POS — facility rates are lower because the facility is separately reimbursed.
→ Facility vs. Non-Facility split in your data
Relative Value Unit (RVU)
The building block of Medicare's fee schedule. Each HCPCS code has physician work RVUs, practice expense RVUs, and malpractice RVUs. Multiplied by the conversion factor (~$33.29 in 2024) to get the allowed amount.
→ The mechanistic driver of your target variable
Coinsurance
The percentage of the allowed amount the patient pays after meeting their deductible. Standard Part B coinsurance is 20%. Some services (preventive care) are 0%.
→ The primary out-of-pocket mechanism for your Stage 2 model
Medigap / Supplemental
Private insurance policies that cover some or all of the cost-sharing that traditional Medicare leaves to the patient. Plans are standardized by letter (A through N). Plan G is the most popular and covers all cost-sharing except the Part B deductible.
→ The single biggest predictor of patient OOP after allowed amount
Section 05

Who Covers the Gap? Supplemental Coverage

The 20% coinsurance that traditional Medicare leaves to patients is the "gap" — and the vast majority of beneficiaries have something that covers part or all of it. This is the most consequential variable for predicting individual out-of-pocket spending, and it's the primary thing MCBS adds to your pipeline.

Coverage TypeWho Has ItWhat It CoversOOP Impact
Medigap Plan G ~28% of FFS beneficiaries; higher-income, older, often urban All coinsurance + Part A deductible. Only gap is Part B annual deductible (~$240) Near-zero OOP
Medigap Plan N Growing share; slightly lower premium than G Same as G but patient pays copays up to $20/visit + $50 ED visits Very low OOP
Employer Retiree ~25% of FFS beneficiaries; varies widely by former employer Variable — some wrap around Medicare generously, others minimally Low–moderate OOP
Medicaid (Dual-Eligible) ~20% of FFS; low-income Medicare beneficiaries Medicaid pays what Medicare doesn't — effectively $0 out of pocket Zero OOP
No Supplement ~10% of FFS beneficiaries; often healthier, younger 65–70 Nothing — patient pays the full 20% coinsurance indefinitely Full 20% OOP
Medicare Advantage ~54% of all Medicare Plan-specific cost-sharing — copays, out-of-pocket maximums, network restrictions Highly variable
The key insight for your Stage 2 model

Supplemental coverage type is more predictive of patient out-of-pocket than the allowed amount itself. A dual-eligible patient having a $5,000 procedure pays $0. An uninsured FFS patient having a $200 procedure pays $40. Coverage type must be a feature — not an afterthought — in your second-stage model.

Section 06

What the CMS Dataset Captures (and Doesn't)

Your primary dataset — the CMS Medicare Physician & Other Practitioners PUF — is a population-level administrative dataset. It is not a sample. It contains every Part B FFS claim submitted by every participating provider for every HCPCS code in a given year, aggregated to protect beneficiary privacy.

What it contains

Per provider × HCPCS × place of service row: the number of services rendered, the number of unique beneficiaries, total submitted charges, Medicare allowed amounts, Medicare payments, average beneficiary risk scores, and provider demographics from NPPES.

What it structurally cannot contain

Missing ElementWhy It's MissingWhy It Matters
Individual patient identitySuppressed for privacy; rows with ≤10 beneficiaries are excludedCan't track a patient's trajectory across providers or over time
Beneficiary cost-sharingClaims capture what Medicare paid, not what the patient paidDirect out-of-pocket is invisible — must be modeled from MCBS

Section 07

What MCBS Adds to the Picture

The Medicare Current Beneficiary Survey (MCBS) is a longitudinal survey of approximately 15,000 Medicare beneficiaries per year, conducted by CMS. It links survey responses to administrative claims, creating the only public dataset that captures both what Medicare paid and what the patient actually paid out of pocket.

Key variables MCBS contributes

Actual Out-of-Pocket Amount
Total annual spending the beneficiary paid themselves across all covered services — the target variable for your Stage 2 model.
Supplemental Coverage Type
Whether the beneficiary has Medigap (and which plan letter), employer retiree, Medicaid dual-eligibility, or no supplement. The most important feature in Stage 2.
Chronic Condition Burden
Number and type of chronic conditions, which correlate with utilization intensity and therefore total out-of-pocket exposure across the year.
Socioeconomic & Demographic
Age, sex, race/ethnicity, income quartile, education — allowing the model to detect equity-relevant patterns in cost-sharing burden.
The resolution mismatch problem

Your CMS data is at state level. MCBS public files are only at census-region level (4 regions) to protect beneficiary privacy. This means Stage 2 geographic granularity is coarser than Stage 1. You'll model "expected OOP for a cardiology patient with Medigap G in the Midwest" — not "in Indiana." This is an acceptable trade-off for a portfolio project, and worth stating clearly.

Why Quantile Regression for Stage 2?

Out-of-pocket spending is heavily right-skewed — most patients pay very little, but a small number with high utilization or no supplement pay a lot. A standard regression predicting the mean isn't very useful here. Quantile regression lets you predict the 10th, 50th, and 90th percentile of OOP simultaneously, giving the end user a range ("you'll likely pay between $0 and $72") rather than a single number that's almost never exactly right.

Section 10

Provider Anomaly Investigation Agent (Phase 9)

The same silver layer that drives allowed-amount prediction also supports a fraud detection track. Phase 9 layers a three-stage detection pipeline and an LLM-powered reasoning agent on top of the existing Medicare pipeline, producing structured investigation briefs for 1.76M unique NPIs across 2013–2023.

Why build this on top of the cost pipeline?

Medicare improper payments run around $47B per year according to HHS. The data to detect a large share of it is already sitting in the silver layer — who billed what, how much, to how many beneficiaries, year over year. The gap between "anomaly detection" and "usable investigation lead" is the hard part: a z-score flag on its own is noise unless an analyst can see the specialty context, patient acuity, trajectory, and which rules actually fit the pattern. That is what the reasoning agent adds.

Pipeline shape

Phase A collapses silver to 11.5M NPI-year profiles with 22 metrics (volume, intensity, Herfindahl, YoY changes, HCC risk score). Phase B runs three detection methods — z-score on log-transformed metrics, Isolation Forest, and temporal rules. Composite flags (≥2 methods agreeing) hit 0.77% of NPI-years. Phase C builds a ProviderContext (profile + specialty benchmarks + top HCPCS + history) and passes it to Claude Sonnet 4.6 along with 10 fraud-indicator rules; the model produces a risk classification (LOW/MEDIUM/HIGH/CRITICAL) and narrative. Phase D adds a Next.js analyst review UI with approve/escalate/dismiss actions and NPI redaction.

The ten fraud indicators

Each brief walks through the same ten rules, with transparent NOT EVALUABLE reasoning when the public dataset can't support a check. The rules are a mix of statistical outliers, regulatory references, and external data joins:

Why transparency about NOT EVALUABLE matters

A fraud detection system that silently skips unchecked rules is worse than one that names them. Every brief disclaims which rules couldn't be evaluated and why — typically because the CMS public PUF is an annual aggregate without date-of-service, claim grouping, or beneficiary-level linkage. Honest scope beats false precision.

The two follow-up rules (UPCODING + LEIE_EXCLUDED)

Two rules that started as NOT EVALUABLE stubs were unlocked by pre-computation and an external data join. UPCODING needed per-NPI E&M code distributions: a one-pass scan over silver produces 5.94M (NPI, year) rows and 863 (specialty, year) benchmark rows with P50/P75/P95 of the 99214+99215 share. The rule fires when a provider's high-tier share exceeds specialty P95 on at least 50 established visits. Roughly 16 of 84 specialties (Orthopedics, Dermatology, Podiatry, Optometry, Urology) have enough spread for the rule to discriminate; the other 68 have the high-tier share saturated at P95=100%, and the rule transparently reports that saturation rather than fabricating a verdict.

LEIE_EXCLUDED joins the OIG List of Excluded Individuals/Entities (~83K rows, ~8,375 with NPI) against flagged providers. An exclusion match is conviction-grade — any Medicare payment to an actively excluded NPI is program-ineligible by law — so it carries CRITICAL severity and overrides the statistical risk ranking.

Interpretive findings from the first 100-brief validation run

The 2023 validation set (top 100 composite-severity flags) surfaced the pattern the agent was built for. The highest-severity flags are dominated by Mass Immunizer Roster Billers and COVID-era supply-code billing — both archetypes of opportunistic volume spikes in a temporary Medicare program. The agent distinguishes these from genuine fraud by reading context: a 9,655% volume spike on Q9967 contrast material with a 1.23 HCC risk score and no prior billing history reads differently than a 300% spike in established patient visits for a provider with a history in the same code family. The brief pushes the ambiguous ones to MEDIUM for analyst review rather than claiming certainty.

Why Claude Sonnet 4.6 and not a rule engine?

A pure rule engine can flag outliers but can't reconcile them against specialty norms, patient acuity, or trajectory. The reasoning the analyst actually needs — "this Internal Medicine provider's 94% high-tier share is technically above P95=92%, but their HCC panel is 1.8 vs. specialty median 1.2, which explains the intensity" — is LLM work, not regex work. The prompt cache carries a 2,584-token system prompt with HCPCS bucket references, specialty norm heuristics, and rule-by-rule interpretive guidance; after the first call in a batch, 2,578 tokens come back as cache reads at ~90% cost savings. Per-brief cost on Sonnet 4.6 comes out to about $0.04.

What the agent is NOT

This is not a fraud determination system. Briefs are investigation leads — structured evidence for a human analyst to decide whether to escalate. No brief concludes fraud definitively. The classification is a prioritization signal, not a legal finding. The analyst UI enforces this by requiring approve/escalate/dismiss decisions on every brief before it moves off the review queue.

Section 11

Honest Limitations of This Approach

A strong portfolio project acknowledges what it can't do. These are the structural limitations that any honest writeup must address.

The Medicare Advantage gap is real and growing

Your entire pipeline operates on Traditional Medicare FFS data. As MA enrollment crosses 54% and continues to grow, your models increasingly describe a minority of Medicare patients. A truly complete system would require access to MA encounter data, which is not publicly available at the procedure level.

MCBS is a sample, not a census

With ~15,000 beneficiaries per year, some cells — particularly rare procedure categories crossed with uncommon coverage types in specific regions — will have very few observations. Confidence intervals will be wide for these edge cases, and the model should communicate that uncertainty rather than hiding it.

Geographic resolution loss in Stage 2

Stage 1 operates at the state level. Stage 2 can only use census-region level geography from MCBS public files. A patient in rural Indiana and a patient in Chicago are both "Midwest" to Stage 2, even though their healthcare markets are dramatically different.

This predicts cost-sharing, not total healthcare cost

The model predicts what a patient will owe for a specific Part B service encounter. It does not predict total annual healthcare spending, doesn't capture inpatient costs (Part A), prescription drug costs (Part D), or the interaction effects between multiple conditions and multiple providers over a year.

Prices change annually

Medicare fee schedules are updated every January. The LSTM helps here for forecasting trends, but any deployed application needs an annual retraining cycle tied to CMS's release calendar (typically mid-year for the prior year's data).
